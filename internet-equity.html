Proceedings of the ACM on Networking · 2026
Less is More: Optimizing Probe Selection Using Shared Latency Anomalies
Taveesh Sharma, Andrew Chu, Paul Schmitt, Francesco Bronzino, Nicole P. Marwell and Nick Feamster
Latency anomalies — persistent or transient increases in round-trip time (RTT) — are a common feature of residential Internet performance. When multiple users simultaneously experience anomalies at the same destination, it may indicate shared infrastructure issues, routing behavior, or congestion. However, inferring such shared behavior is challenging in practice, because the magnitude of these anomalies can vary significantly across devices even within the same ISP and geographic area, and detailed network topology information is often unavailable due to platform limitations or privacy constraints. This paper studies whether devices that experience a shared latency anomaly observe similar changes in RTT magnitude using a topology-agnostic approach. Using a four-month dataset of high-frequency RTT measurements from 99 residential probes in Chicago, the authors detect shared anomalies and analyze their consistency in amplitude and duration without relying on traceroutes or explicit path information. They find that many shared anomalies affect users similarly in amplitude, particularly within the same ISP, and develop a sampling algorithm that reduces redundancy in detected anomalies by selecting representative devices under user-defined constraints. The approach covers 95% of aggregate anomaly impact with less than half the total probes used in the deployment.

Beyond Data Points: Regionalizing Crowdsourced Latency Measurements
Taveesh Sharma, Paul Schmitt, Francesco Bronzino, Nicole P. Marwell and Nick Feamster
Internet performance measurements are typically analyzed at fine-grained geographic levels, but this approach can obscure broader spatial patterns in service quality. This paper develops a regionalization method for crowdsourced latency data, grouping geographic areas by similarity in their internet performance characteristics. Applied to data from a major U.S. city, the approach reveals coherent regional patterns that cut across administrative boundaries and correlate with underlying network infrastructure, offering a new lens for understanding and addressing internet inequity.

Are We Up to the Challenge? An Analysis of the FCC Broadband Data Collection Fixed Internet Availability Challenges
Jonatas Marques, Alexis Schrubbe, Nicole P. Marwell and Nick Feamster
The FCC Broadband Data Collection (BDC) introduced a formal challenge process allowing entities to dispute reported internet availability. This paper provides the first systematic analysis of how that challenge process has functioned in practice, examining who files challenges, what geographic areas they target, and how successful they are. The findings reveal significant gaps in the challenge process that may limit its ability to correct inaccuracies in the BDC data.

The Hitchhiker’s Guide to Analyzing the FCC Broadband Data Collection
Jonatas Marques, Alexis Schrubbe, Nicole P. Marwell and Nick Feamster
The FCC Broadband Data Collection represents the most comprehensive federal effort to map internet availability in the United States, but working with the data presents significant methodological challenges. This paper provides a practical guide to the structure, limitations, and analytical possibilities of the BDC dataset, offering researchers and policymakers the tools they need to use the data effectively and responsibly.
TPRC · 2023
A First Look at the Spatial and Temporal Variability of Internet Performance Data in Hyperlocal Geographies
Taveesh Sharma, Jonatas Marques, Nick Feamster and Nicole P. Marwell
Internet performance can vary substantially over short distances and time periods, yet most research aggregates measurements at relatively coarse geographic scales. This paper examines the spatial and temporal variability of internet performance data at hyperlocal levels — census blocks and sub-block geographies — revealing patterns of inequality that larger-scale analyses may miss, and raising important questions about the appropriate unit of analysis for broadband equity research.
ACM SIGMETRICS · 2023
A Comparative Analysis of Ookla Speedtest and Measurement Lab’s Network Diagnostic Test (NDT7)
Kyle MacMillan, Tarun Mangla, James Saxon, Nicole P. Marwell and Nick Feamster
Speed tests are widely used to measure internet performance, but different tools can yield very different results. This paper offers the first rigorous side-by-side comparison of two of the most commonly used speed test platforms — Ookla Speedtest and Measurement Lab’s NDT7 — examining how they differ in methodology, user base, and measured outcomes, and what those differences mean for research and policy applications.

TPRC · 2022
Internet Inequity in Chicago: Adoption, Affordability, and Availability
Kyle MacMillan, Tarun Mangla, Nick Feamster and Nicole P. Marwell
This paper documents the dimensions of internet inequality in Chicago across three domains: adoption (who has internet service), affordability (who can afford adequate service), and availability (where fast service is offered). Using a combination of survey data, FCC data, and network measurements, the paper shows that these three dimensions do not always align, and that addressing internet inequity requires policies targeted at each dimension separately.
TPRC · 2022
Best Practices for Collecting Speed Test Data
Kyle MacMillan, Tarun Mangla, Nick Feamster and Nicole P. Marwell
Speed test data is widely used in broadband policy and research, but the quality and comparability of such data depends heavily on how it is collected. This paper develops and evaluates a set of best practices for speed test data collection, addressing questions of sampling, instrumentation, timing, and reporting that can significantly affect the validity of findings.
TPRC · 2022
Benchmarks or Equity? A New Approach to Measuring Internet Performance
Nick Feamster and Nicole P. Marwell
Current approaches to measuring internet performance focus on whether service meets fixed technical benchmarks, such as minimum upload and download speeds. This paper argues that such benchmark-based approaches fail to capture the internet equity dimensions of performance, and proposes an equity-centered framework for measurement that considers who gets what quality of service relative to others in their community and city.
